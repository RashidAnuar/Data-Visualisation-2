How Poverty and Unemployment Interact Across Malaysia

This visualisation examines how poverty and unemployment rate evolve across Malaysia's state and over time, revealing patterns of economic vulnerability and resilience. By analysing poverty metrics (absolute, relative and hardcore poverty) alongside unemployment data, we uncovering how the economic vulnerability manifest across states and time periods

Use the available controls to navigate patterns

  • Metric dropdowns needs to be selected for interactive viewing
  • Year Sliders focus on a period which update the chart instantly
  • State Selection allows to inspect a state's trend

Metric dropdown — choose the poverty measure that colors the map

Poverty Metric

  • Absolute poverty — Household income is below the Poverty Line Income (PLI) ; can't meet basic needs (food, housing, utilities, transport).
  • Hardcore poverty — Income is below the Food PLI ; can't meet minimum calorie/food needs. Subset of absolute poverty.
  • Relative poverty — Income is below 50% of the state's median ; indicates deprivation vs local living standards.

How to read: darker color = higher rate. Rates shown for the selected year; methods may change across years (e.g., revised PLI).

    Does poverty track unemployment?

    Short answer: Yes—states with higher poverty generally have higher unemployment, but the strength of the link varies across the range and through time.

    How to read this chart:

    • Rightward = higher poverty; upward = higher unemployment.
    • The red line is a fitted trend summarizing the average relationship.
    • Dots far above the line have unusually high unemployment for their poverty level; dots below the line are doing better than expected.

    What this chart is saying

    • Positive association: Unemployment tends to increase as poverty increases which is in line with the idea that deprivation and poor local labour market coexists
    • Nonlinear pattern: The trend flattens at low and high poverty before it steepens in the mid-range, suggesting that other factors limiting unemployment
    • Spread matters: Some states have extreme lower unemployment rate at similar poverty levels, which may be due to differences in industrial mix, migration or policy efficacy

    Hover — over a point to see the state and year of the observation and it consequent values

    Timing & persistence: When does it surge?

    • Recurring hotspots: Sabah and Sarawak remain darker than most states across decades, indicating inherently higher rates.
    • Crisis surges: The map is noticeably darker in 1984–1990 and 2019–2021 after these surges the cells lighten, indicating recovery.
    • Low phase: Many states lighten notably during 2012–2016, suggesting a trough across metrics.
    • Urban centres: Kuala Lumpur and Putrajaya remain among the lightest cells for most years.
    • Metric behavior: Poverty typically lasts longer than unemployment, suggesting a slower rate of household recovery than the labor market recovery. Unemployment, on the other hand, exhibits brief, rapid surges.

    Labour market shock & recoveries

    • 1987-1989 surge: Unemployment spikes across almost all states, with several states (e.g., Terengganu, Perak, Sarawak, Sabah,) peaking well above their long-run levels. Many series roughly double from ~3 to 5% to ~6 to 12% before easing.
    • Early 1990s recovery: Rates fall quickly after 1990, stabilising around ~2 to 4% for most states.
    • COVID-19 shock (2020-2021): A shorter spike appears nationwide. Most states rise by ~1 to 2 percentage points (peaks around ~4 to 5%) and then fall again by 2022.
    • Baseline differences: Sabah/Sarawak tend to sit on the higher side; Kuala Lumpur/Putrajaya are among the lowest throughout.

    In 1985-1986 the Malaysian economy saw contractions in commodities, public spending, and early manufacturing, resulting in a significant increase in unemployment (see chart).

    Do Poverty & Unemployment move together?

    • Common Movement: From 1995-2016, unemployment and absolute/relative poverty trend down together.
    • Shock response: Unemployment shows short spikes (e.g., 2020-2022), while absolute poverty have sharp spikes. However relative poverty increase slightly
    • Relative poverty is stickier: It declines more gradually and sits above absolute poverty across decades.
    • Takeaway: There's a moderate positive correlation over time, but the series react on differently over time

    The Sudden Surge (2016-2019) in absolute poverty
    In 2019, the Malaysian government updated the PLI to RM2,208 per month based on a new methodology. This new line was more realistic and aligned with international standards. Thus causes a statistical surge by correcting millions more Malaysian living in poverty
